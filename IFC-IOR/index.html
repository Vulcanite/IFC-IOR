IFC-IOR
Collaboration for Safety and Security

To promote collaborative maritime safety and security in the direction of a peaceful, stable, and prosperous Indian Ocean Region, the IFC-IOR was established to be a nodal centre of excellence and acts as a maritime security information sharing hub for the region.

More than 80% of the world's seaborne oil trade traverses through the Indian Ocean and its primary chokepoints, which include 40% through the Strait of Hormuz, 35% through the Strait of Malacca, and 8% through the Bab el-Mandeb. These sea lanes are among the most strategically significant ones in the world. In addition, the IOR exists to be utilised by 75% of global marine traffic. Situational awareness of marine activities is necessary to combat threats including maritime terrorism, piracy and armed robbery, human and drug trafficking, illegal, unreported, and unreported (IUU) fishing, gun smuggling, and poaching. There was a need for regional collaboration to enable information exchange, cooperation, and information development. IFC-IOR was designed to meet this specific purpose. There was a need for collaboration across regions to enable sharing of information, cooperation, and expertise development. In consideration of the Indian Ocean Region's significance in terms of global trade and security, IFC-IOR was envisaged to meet this specific requirement for promoting collaboration for maritime safety and security.

Establishment

IFC-IOR, hosted by the Indian Navy, was established by the Government of India at Gurugram on 22 Dec 18.
